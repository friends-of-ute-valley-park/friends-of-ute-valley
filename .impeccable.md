## Design Context

### Users

Primary users are current and prospective volunteers deciding whether to show up, stay informed, and participate in stewardship work at Ute Valley Park. Secondary users are local park visitors checking trail conditions, rules, trailheads, park hours, and other practical visit details. Additional users include donors, partners, and community advocates who need confidence that Friends of Ute Valley Park is credible, active, and locally rooted.

These users are often making quick decisions on mobile or from a community-news context, so the interface should make action paths obvious and practical information easy to scan. The core job to be done is helping people understand the park, trust the organization, and take the next concrete step to support or protect the wildland.

### Brand Personality

The brand should feel trustworthy, civic, and grounded. It should evoke calm confidence, local stewardship, and practical credibility rather than hype or trend-chasing.

The voice should be clear, direct, and community-minded. Write like a capable local stewardship organization: factual, welcoming, and organized, with enough warmth to feel volunteer-powered but never so casual that it weakens trust.

### Aesthetic Direction

Preserve the established light-mode-first visual system. The site already points toward an editorial field-guide and civic-report hybrid: stone neutrals, forest greens, teals, and amber accents; real park photography; strong borders and grids; bold serif headlines; monospaced metadata; and clean sans-serif body copy.

Lean into real imagery, maps, trail data, park terminology, and concrete status information to create authenticity. The experience should feel specific to Colorado Springs and Ute Valley Park, not like a generic nonprofit template or a polished corporate microsite.

Anti-direction: do not drift into overly corporate, overly slick institutional, or generic SaaS/nonprofit aesthetics. Also avoid whimsical styling, faux-rustic camp branding, or decorative choices that make stewardship feel less credible.

### Design Principles

1. Prioritize volunteer action and orientation first, especially when tradeoffs arise between storytelling and utility.
2. Build trust through specificity: use real dates, park conditions, trailhead details, impact metrics, and operational clarity.
3. Let the landscape carry emotion through authentic photography and restrained, nature-rooted color rather than decorative excess.
4. Use strong typographic hierarchy with editorial contrast: bold serif headlines, monospaced labels and metadata, and highly readable body text.
5. Keep interactions accessible and low-friction with clear focus states, reduced-motion respect, simple forms, and scan-friendly layouts.
